## Why a simple rule helps

Most people do not fail at money because they are bad at math. They fail because money disappears before anyone decides where it should go. The **50/30/20 rule** is a starting map: about half for needs, about a third for wants, and the rest for saving and debt payoff.

It is not a law. It is a lens. When you know the lens, you can adjust it for your real life — part-time income, allowance plus gig work, or support from family.

<ConceptCard title="Needs (~50%)" graphic="need50">
  Housing, basic food, transport to school or work, required fees, medicine, and minimum debt payments you must make
  to avoid serious consequences.
</ConceptCard>

<ConceptCard title="Wants (~30%)" graphic="want30">
  Streaming, hobbies, eating out, nicer clothes than the minimum, games, and anything you could postpone without losing
  safety or opportunity.
</ConceptCard>

<ConceptCard title="Savings & goals (~20%)" graphic="save20">
  Emergency fund, investing, extra debt payments beyond minimums, and money you move toward named goals like a laptop or
  a trip.
</ConceptCard>

## Worked example

Say you earn **$400** in a month from a weekend job.

- **Needs (50%)**: $200 for transport, phone plan you rely on, and helping with food.
- **Wants (30%)**: $120 for social plans, hobbies, and small treats.
- **Savings (20%)**: $80 toward an emergency buffer or a bigger purchase.

If your needs are higher than 50% because of where you live, you temporarily shift the ratio — but you still label the
spending so you can see the pressure clearly.

## Irregular income

If you babysit sometimes and tutor other months, average your last few months, use a conservative guess, and still run
the ratio on that number. When you earn more than expected, resist the urge to upgrade every want at once — split the
surprise between savings and a single intentional treat.

<RealWorldExample>
  Many financial educators treat 50/30/20 as a training wheel, not a tattoo. The goal is awareness: you can tighten wants
  during exam season, or raise savings when you pick up extra shifts.
</RealWorldExample>
