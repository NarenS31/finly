## The trading problem

Long ago, people **bartered**: you might trade eggs for shoes. That works sometimes, but it breaks down fast. What if
the shoemaker does not want eggs today? What if your eggs spoil before you find a trade?

Money became a shared middle language. Instead of matching wants exactly, people trade for coins, notes, or numbers in
an account — then trade those for what they need later.

<ConceptCard title="Medium of exchange" graphic="exchange">
  Money is something widely accepted so you do not need a perfect double match of goods.
</ConceptCard>

<ConceptCard title="Store of value" graphic="store">
  Money lets you save effort for later — if prices stay stable enough that what you saved still buys something useful.
</ConceptCard>

## Different shapes of money

Money can be metal coins, paper bills, or digits on a screen. In many places, adults tap cards or phones. The important
idea is not the object — it is the **agreement** in a community that this thing counts.

<FunFact>
  The first coins appeared thousands of years ago in ancient kingdoms. Leaders stamped marks so people could trust the
  metal weight without weighing every trade.
</FunFact>

## Where money comes from for kids and teens

You might earn money from allowance, chores, helping neighbors, selling something you made, or a part-time job when you
are old enough. In every case, money usually shows up because you **provided value** — time, skill, or something useful.

## Digital money is still money

When adults say money moved to their “account,” it is still backed by rules, banks, and governments that try to keep the
system fair and stable. Your job is to learn how to **earn, save, spend, and share** with care — no matter what form the
money takes.
