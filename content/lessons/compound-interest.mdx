## Why this matters

Imagine two people save the same amount. One starts at 14, the other starts at 20. The first person often ends up with much more money. Why? Compound interest.

<ConceptCard title="Compound Interest" icon="📈">
  Compound interest means you earn interest on your original money and on the
  interest you already earned. Your money can grow faster over time.
</ConceptCard>

## Simple vs compound interest

Simple interest only pays interest on the starting amount.

Compound interest pays interest on the starting amount plus previous interest.

Formula:

$A = P(1 + r/n)^{nt}$

Where:

- $A$ is final amount
- $P$ is principal (starting money)
- $r$ is annual rate
- $n$ is times interest is added per year
- $t$ is years

<RealWorldExample>
  If you save $100 and earn 10% simple interest, you get $10 each year. With
  compound interest, year 2 earns interest on $110, not just $100.
</RealWorldExample>

<RealWorldExample>
  Warren Buffett often says his wealth snowballed because compounding had decades to run — most of the dollars showed up
  late, after patience did the heavy lifting.
</RealWorldExample>

## Compounding helps savings and harms debt

If you save regularly, compounding helps you.
If you carry high-interest debt, compounding works against you.
